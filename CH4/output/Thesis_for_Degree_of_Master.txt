ABSTRACT
Comparison of Model Selection Criteria for
Neighborhood Selection Method in the
Gaussian Graphical Models
Ko, Keon
Department of Applied Statistics
Graduate School of Konkuk University
For the gaussian graphical models(GGMs), penalized estimators are gener-
ally used in the high-dimensional situation. Representative estimator is neigh-
borhood selection which is penalized regression based method. So there is the
model selection problem for the neighborhood selection method. In this paper,
we explore various model selection criteria based on the generalized informa-
tion criterion(GIC) for the neighborhood selection method in the GGMs by the
simulation study.
keyword : Gaussian graphical model, Model selection criterion, Neighborhood selec-
tion.
iv
Chapter 1. Introduction
Graphical models are a probability model of multivariate variables, they are
related to graph. The graphical models represent the relationships among the
variables visually, it can be applied widely where the relationships are severely
entangled. The relationships are expressed by edge of the graph, the edges
related to the conditional dependencies between the nodes which correspond
to variables.
Furthermore if the random variables follow multivariate gaussian distri-
bution, then it is gaussian graphical models (GGMs). In the GGMs, zero en-
tries of the inverse covariance matrix correspond to conditional dependencies
between the random variables given all other variables. This problem is co-
variance selection (Dempster, 1972), related studies still continues these days.
Especially under the high-dimensional situation where the number of vari-
ables p is greater than the sample size n, the sample covariance matrix is singu-
lar. Therefore it is hard to get the inverse covariance matrix estimate. So vari-
ous methods have been introduced for the high dimensional situation, mainly
there are two approaches.
One of approaches is regression based method. Neighborhood selection
method (Meinshausen and Bühlmann, 2006) proposed to identify the nonzero
elements of the inverse covariance matrix by performing variable by variable
LASSO regression (Tibshirani, 1996). Peng et al. (2009) proposed a joint re-
gression model which simultaneously performs regression for all variables.
Dantzig selector based estimator and CLIME estimator are suggested in Yuan
(2010) and Cai et al. (2011). The proposed methods are implemented via linear
programming.
Another estimation method is penalized log-likelihood method which di-
rectly estimates the inverse covariance matrix. Yuan and Lin (2007) proposed
penalized likelihood method for model selection and estimation.
To solve
the optimization problem, they used maxdet algorithm. Banerjee et al. (2008)
solved the optimization problem of the estimator which was proposed Yuan
1
and Lin (2007) by using block coordinate descent algorithm. Based on ap-
proach in Banerjee et al. (2008), Friedman, Hastie, and Tibshirani (2008) pro-
posed the Graphical LASSO (GLASSO) algorithm. Mazumder et al. (2012) an-
alyze the theoretical properties of GLASSO. Under the penalized likelihood,
non-convex penalties also have been considered by Fan and Feng (2009) in-
stead of L1-penalty.
Most of the estimators mentioned above need to select the tuning param-
eters. Depending on which tuning parameter is selected, the complexity of
the graph is different. Thus, tuning parameter selection is also important is-
sue in the GGMs. However, there are few studies on the tuning parameters
compared to estimation. Foygel and Drton (2010) studied the consistency of
extended BIC (EBIC) for GGMs where both p and sample size increase. Their
simulation gives the results of GLASSO with EBIC. Gao et al. (2012) stud-
ied the consistency of the BIC for penalized MLE with SCAD. And they also
considered the consistency of modified BIC when p tends to infinity as sam-
ple size increases. Liu and Wang (2017) proposed TIGER which used penal-
ized regression with SQRT-LASSO. It is asymptotically tuning free and non-
asymptotically tuning insensitive. Recently, an estimator based on the gener-
alized information criterion (Konishi and Kitagawa, 1996) has been proposed
in Abbruzzo et al. (2019).
In this paper, we focus on the comparing the model selection criteria based
on the generalized information criterion (Konishi and Kitagawa, 1996; Kim et
al., 2012) for the neighborhood selection method in the GGMs. For this pur-
pose, first, we review the graphical models and the gaussian graphical models
which follow the multivariate gaussian distribution. Next, we introduce the
neighborhood selection method which is the penalized regression based esti-
mator. Also we introduce the model selection criterion for the neighborhood
selection. In addition, we provide simulation studies to compare the various
information criteria for the neighborhood selection.
The remain of this paper is organized as follows. Chapter 2, we briefly in-
troduce the graphical models. Also we review the gaussian graphical models
which is the representative graphical models. Chapter 3, we describe regres-
sion based estimator and its model selection criteria. Chapter 4, we give some
2
simulation studies comparing with the performance of the neighborhood se-
lection method with different model selection criteria.

Chapter 2. Graphical Models: Review
In this section, we review the graphical models briefly. For the full details
about the theory, recommend to see the following papers: Lauritzen (1996),
Drton and Maathuis (2017), and Maathuis et al. (2018).
1.
Graphical Models
A graphical model is useful tool which can represent the conditional depen-
dencies among the random variables by using the graph (Lauritzen, 1996). Let
G = (V, E) be a graph, where V = {1, . . . , p} is the set of nodes and E is the set
of edges in V × V. In the graphical models, the nodes correspond to random
variables and the edges represent the conditional dependencies between the
variables (Drton and Maathuis, 2017). The graphical models can be either di-
rected or undirected, depending on which way the edge is pointing. In other
words, suppose the adjacency matrix A = {aij}, if the A is symmetric(i.e.,
aij = aji), it is an undirected graph and if aij ̸= aji, it is directed graph. There-
fore if we look at the relationship between variables through an undirected
graph, it is called an undirected graphical models. While if we represent the
relationship associated with causality through the directed graph, it is referred
to a directed graphical models.
When an undirected graph is given, then we can estimate the structure of
undirected graph. Briefly there are two types of graph which is the marginal
correlation graphs and the conditional independent graphs. The marginal cor-
relation graph is the correlation between two variables. On the other hand,
the conditional independent graph is the partial correlation between two vari-
ables given a set of random variables. In this paper, we focus on the undirected
graphical models and the conditional independent graph.
Let X = (X1, . . . , Xp)T be a p-dimensional random vector correspond to the
set of nodes V in the undirected graph G. A random vector X satisfies three
different Markov properties with respect to G (Lauritzen, 1996; Bühlmann and
4
Van De Geer, 2011; Drton and Maathuis, 2017): pairwise, local, and global.
A random vector X satisfies the pairwise Markov property relative to G if
Xi ⊥Xj|XV\{i,j}

which do not contain nodes i and j. And we can say that X satisfies the local
Markov property relative to G if
Xi ⊥{Xk; k ∈V\{i, a ∈N(i)}}|XN(i)
(1)
for all k ∈V, where N(i) is the set of neighbors of node i. And all nodes
included in N(i) are connected to node i. Let A, B and C be the subsets of
V, X satisfies global Markov property relative to G if for any disjoint subsets
(A, B, C) such that C separates A from B in G
XA ⊥XB|XC.
The three different Markov properties are equivalent and the proposition is
proved in Lauritzen (1996).

Chapter 3. Estimation of the conditional
independence
Representative estimator with GGMs is neighborhood selection method (Mein-
shausen and Bühlmann, 2006) which is regression based estimator. Neighbor-
hood selection performs penalized regression separately column by column.
By performing the penalized regression, we can estimate conditional inde-
pendence. Also through the existence of the edge, it is possible to identify
the graphical model. In this section, we introduce the neighborhood selection
models briefly.
1.
Neighborhood selection
Neighborhood selection method estimates the zeros in the inverse covariance
matrix by fitting a penalized regression that each variable used as the response
and the rest used as the predictors. The goal of neighborhood selection is es-
timating the neighborhoods of given variables. Neighborhood selection con-
siders the Local Markov property and the multivariate gaussian distribution
property.
First, we recall N(i) which is the set of neighbors of a node i. All nodes
included in N(i) are connected to node i. When a node i’s neighborhood N(i)
is given, node i is conditional independent all the other variables except node
i and N(i), i.e. (1). From (3), the nonzero regression coefficients for the true
regression model of Xi is same for the variables that contained in the set of
neighborhoods, so the set of neighborhoods N(i) can be written as
N(i) = {j ∈V : βij ̸= 0}.
(6)

Xi and predictors {Xj : j ∈V\{i}} in linear regression (Meinshausen and
Bühlmann, 2006; Maathuis et al., 2018).

2.
Model selection criterion for neighborhood selection
As model selection is required for high dimensional data modeling, various
model selection criteria have been proposed. The well-known model selection
criteria such as the AIC (Akaike, 1998), the BIC (Schwarz, 1978) are introduced
for a long time. Cross-validation also commonly used for the simplicity but it
is known to overfit as the AIC.
9
The generalized information criterion (GIC) has been considered in Kon-
ishi and Kitagawa (1996), and Kim et al. (2012). The GIC family includes not
only AIC, BIC but also other information criteria. Especially, Kim et al. (2012)
studied sufficient conditions of many information criteria based on the GIC.
The tuning parameter controls the sparsity of the estimator in the neighbor-
hood selection. To choose the tuning parameter, we used the model selection
criterion based on the GIC. When σ is unknown, we may use the mean squared

• For the BIC, in the case of LASSO, SEN increases where n = p when

Chapter 5. Conclusion
In this paper, we studied diverse model selection criteria based on the GIC for
the neighborhood selection by the simulation study. Depending on the n and
p, the GICs indicate different results and patterns in terms of sensitivity and
specificity. The advantage of comparison of various model selection criteria
lies in its diversity of choices about how to select the tuning parameters under
the specific n and p. However, there is a lack of theoretical properties about
the selection consistency for the GGMs. And this study is limited to the neigh-
borhood selection method. We believe that comparison of GICs for another
penalized estimator, for example GLASSO, is valuable.
